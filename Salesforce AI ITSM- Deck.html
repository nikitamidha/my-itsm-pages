Vision & where ITSM is headed
01 · Vision & Industry Trends

The shift is not better ticketing — it's resolving issues before the customer notices

Traditional ITSM starts after customer pain: report, triage, diagnose, dispatch, recover. AI-native ITSM starts earlier: agents monitor signals, detect anomalies, diagnose causes, attempt reversible fixes, assemble evidence, and escalate only when judgment, risk, or physical work demands it.

The structural move

From episodic & ticket-led → always-on, predictive, preventive

The old world is episodic, ticket-led, and human-processed. The new world is always-on and governed by humans where judgment, risk, trust, security, or customer context matters.

The real constraint

Agentic AI has crossed from theory to execution — but trust gates adoption

Everyone now sells a "digital workforce." The differentiating question is no longer can agents act, but what will enterprises let agents do?

Competitive frame

Governed workflow vs. reasoning in context

ServiceNow — governed workflow execution: powerful for repeatable work, less flexible when incidents cross domains.3  Salesforce / Agentforce — agents that reason, act, observe and adapt in the same environment as customer, service, field and collaboration context.4 Real incidents rarely stay inside one workflow.

First principles & product outlook
02 · Principles & Product Outlook

First principles that define the product

Agents don't just do the repeatable work — agents do the work. Humans manage the moments and judgment that matter. Each principle is a design constraint that makes that division safe.

① Proactivity is designed in

If the system sees risk first and does not act, the product failed. Monitoring is the front door of service.

② Humans manage the AI workforce

CSAs don't chase status, IT Ops don't repeat safe diagnostics, Field Techs don't arrive blind.

③ Confidence & risk drive escalation

Escalate on uncertainty, irreversibility, blast radius, compliance exposure, and relationship risk.

④ Documentation creates the learning loop

A structured trail of observations, actions, confidence, approvals, changes, and messages — auditable and reusable.

⑤ Governance is the moat

Autonomy starts narrow and reversible, then expands only with proven performance. Every action is logged and overridable.

⑥ The unit of design is the client relationship

SLA tier, tone, risk tolerance, site criticality, and end-customer impact shape every agent decision.

ITSM agent workforce
03 · ITSM Agent Workforce

ITSM agent workforce in two layers — each agent is a role, not a feature

Agents do the operational work; humans manage the moments that matter. The workforce splits into a shared platform layer and a specialist (domain) layer — and every agent runs to the same contract.

Platform layer · shared agents

Run the whole fleet, before a client reports pain

Proactive: Monitoring & Detection · Asset Discovery & Inventory · Preventive Maintenance · Impact & Correlation.

Reactive: Scoping & Triage · Router · Field Dispatch · Communication — the same shape on every ticket across all three service lines.

Specialist layer · domain agents

Adapt the pattern to each service line

CCTV: Footage Integrity · Diagnostic.  Web hosting: Deployment Watch · Resolution.

Network: Baseline Monitor · Diagnostic — circuit baselines, ISP evidence, remote fixes, and field handoff.

Human-in-the-loop framework
05 · HITL & Action Modes

Human-in-the-loop is the management system, not a fallback

Agents act autonomously by default. A human enters in a defined action mode — triggered by confidence, reversibility, blast radius, compliance exposure, or relationship risk.

Autonomous

Agent senses, diagnoses, runs a reversible runbook and verifies — no human. High confidence, low blast radius.

Watch

Human monitors autonomous work to time the client update; acts only if the agent flags it.

Inputs Required

Agent pauses and asks the human for a missing decision, fact, or access it can't obtain itself.

On-site

Physical access & judgment agents can't provide — e.g. the demarc test the backend can't run.

Approval

Human governs a hard-to-reverse or high-blast-radius change before an agent may execute it.

Escalate / Cluster

Novel signals, low-confidence cases, or cross-client clusters routed to the right human or specialist.

Verify & Close

Accountable human reviews the resolved incident, approves compliance-sensitive comms, and signs off.

Calibrate & Tune

Humans fine-tune agents and adjust autonomy thresholds over time as performance is proven.

Sarah → Watch · Inputs Required · Approval · Verify & Close  |  Marcus → Approval · Escalate · Calibrate  |  Ray → On-site. One incident may touch zero, one, or several modes depending on risk.

11 · Roadmap

What Comes Next

Three capability phases beyond the current prototype — autonomy expands only with proven performance.

Phase 1
Predictive Operations
Find and fix issues before they occur
  • Anomaly pattern detection across telemetry history
  • Asset health trending to predict failure weeks ahead
  • Risk-triggered maintenance, not scheduled
Phase 2
Governance & Control
Configurable levers for agent autonomy
  • Per-client, per-action autonomy settings
  • Permissions that widen as agents earn a track record
  • Audit trail and human override at every level
Phase 3
Specialization & Learning
Agents that know your specific deployment
  • Device-family specialists by model and circuit type
  • Client-specific baselines that build over time
  • Depth that compounds per deployment, not resets

Architecture & technical considerations
12 · Architecture

Three layers of technical considerations

Product constraints set the boundaries; Salesforce provides context; the agent layer executes. Each layer has its own design decisions.

Layer 1
Product Constraints
Confidence gates - each action type has a threshold; below it the agent escalates rather than acts.
Reversibility constraint - agents may only take irreversible actions after explicit approval. Per-asset frequency caps prevent runaway automation loops.
Audit trail - every article read, action taken, and escalation decision is logged.
Layer 2
Context Layer — Salesforce
Structured data queriet at runtime — asset registry (Configuration Management Database), client SLAs, and telemetry are queried live, not baked into the model. This helps ensure no stale knowledge.
MCP servers — external integrations (monitoring platforms, ISP APIs) connect via Model Context Protocol, giving agents permissioned tool access without open credentials.
Per-client isolation — access token scoping at the data layer enforces client boundaries deterministically. An agent serving Client A cannot read or write Client B data, regardless of prompt.
Layer 3
Agent Layer — Knowledge & Guardrails
RAG, not fine-tuning — operational knowledge (runbooks, device specs, firmware notes) lives in a retrieval index. Updates publish immediately without retraining. Fine-tuning was ruled out because keeping a model current with a changing device fleet and deployment landscape is impractical.
Partitioned RAG — agents are pre-scoped to the relevant partition using deterministic fields (e.g. camera model + firmware version from the asset registry + client ID). This bounds search space and prevents cross-domain retrieval noise.
Precision vs. ambiguity — asset state, action parameters, and client-facing disclosures require absolute accuracy and go through HITL. Internal diagnostic summaries and draft communications tolerate more latitude.
Evals — agents are tested against historical incidents with known outcomes. Regressions on triage classification, action selection, or escalation trigger block deployment.
Strategic positioning

Proactive, governed, human-in-the-loop service operations

Position this as AI-native managed service operations — not generic ITSM automation. Fewer customer-reported issues, governed AI agents plus human managers, and field service as part of one continuous, context-rich loop.
Agents do the repeatable work. Humans manage the moments that matter.
